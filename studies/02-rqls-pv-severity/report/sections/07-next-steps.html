Next Steps & References

This study's job — quantify, against a known truth, what robust quantile least squares costs and buys for a PV loss-severity premium under contamination and incomplete data — is done. The open questions for a follow-up, in priority order (from findings.md §7):

  1. Model the mixture (per-peril fits) — the structural fix. Every finding points here: the 23–42% underpricing at ε=0 (E6, E7) is misspecification, not estimation. Fit each peril (or a lognormal mixture with a heavy hail component) separately, price the layer on the mixture, and re-run the E7 grid. Prediction to falsify: the ε=0 column collapses toward the 4.28% floor and the "stopped-clock" cancellation disappears.
  2. An MLE-tc + window-QLS ensemble. Trunc-MLE won the realistic cell (7.85%, E5) but is slow and less contamination-immune at high ε; window-QLS is fast and bounded. A blend may dominate both across the ε grid — target the best per-cell error in the decision table.
  3. An ε-contamination stress-test template for filings. Package the E3/E7 sweep harness as a reusable exhibit: given a fitted severity model and a layer, report the signed premium / VaR / TVaR error across ε ∈ {0,1,2,5,10}% and flag any cancellation cell. This turns the study's method into a standard robustness appendix for a rate filing.
  4. Acquire real PV loss-severity data. The lab is calibrated to illustrative PV shape, not a real book. Even a modest real dataset (deductible/limit-layered claims) would let the estimators be compared on genuine severity — with the synthetic lab as the known-truth control for what "recovered the truth" should look like.
  5. GLS with the full covariance on coarser grids. The diagonal plug-in GLS added noise at 19 points (E2). The off-diagonal Σ−1 is the efficiency ceiling but ill-conditions on fine grids; a coarser 7–9 point grid with the full covariance may finally beat OLS.

Verified references

Carried from the study's method_card.md §5, each verified against the primary source (arXiv abstract and/or publisher) during the METHOD gate on 2026-07-10. The QLS estimator and the OLS/GLS quantile weighting come from Adjieteh & Brazauskas 2025; the truncation/censoring framework from Poudyal & Brazauskas 2022 and Poudyal 2021; the method of trimmed moments from Brazauskas, Jones & Zitikis 2009.

  • Adjieteh, M. & Brazauskas, V. (2025). Quantile Least Squares: A Flexible Approach for Robust Estimation and Validation of Location-Scale Families. Statistics and Computing 35, Art. 106. — DOI 10.1007/s11222-025-10626-6; arXiv:2402.07837. The QLS estimator, the joint asymptotic normality of sample quantiles, and the OLS/GLS weighting.
  • Poudyal, C. & Brazauskas, V. (2022). Robust Estimation of Loss Models for Truncated and Censored Severity Data. Variance 15(2). — arXiv:2202.13000. The truncation / censoring / scaling framework the window fit rests on.
  • Brazauskas, V., Jones, B. & Zitikis, R. (2009). Robust fitting of claim severity distributions and the method of trimmed moments. Journal of Statistical Planning and Inference 139(6), 2028–2043. — the MTM cousin measured here.
  • Poudyal, C. (2021). Robust Estimation of Loss Models for Lognormal Insurance Payment Severity Data. ASTIN Bulletin 51(2). — DOI 10.1017/asb.2021.4; arXiv:2103.02089. The lognormal payment-severity robustness results.

Honesty note (carried from the method card). No published paper applies QLS to photovoltaic risk — the PV bridge is this study's original construction. The QLS / MTM / truncated-MLE methods and their asymptotics are from the peer-reviewed severity-modeling literature above; grafting them onto a PV loss-severity generator (peril mix, hail concentration, fire GPD tail) is our own synthetic lab, built to make the estimators' behaviour visible against a known truth. The PV loss-concentration figures that motivate the peril mix are industry-reported (pv-magazine 2025) and used as market context only — not validated model parameters.
