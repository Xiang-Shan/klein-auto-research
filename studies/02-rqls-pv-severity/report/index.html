The Question

Picture the moment this study exists for. You are an actuary handed a book of photovoltaic (solar) property risk to price — and there is no loss history. The technology is young, the portfolio younger, and the claims that would tell you how severe a hail strike or an inverter fire really gets have not been filed yet. Yet a premium is due. Where do the numbers even come from, and once a trickle of real claims does arrive — dirty, fat-fingered, clipped by deductibles and limits — which estimator do you trust to turn them into a rate?

Finding out on real data is impossible here: there is no real data with a known answer to check yourself against. So this study does the next best thing an experimentalist can do — it builds a known-truth synthetic lab. A generator produces PV losses whose exact risk-loaded premium under the product layer is known by construction, so every estimator can be scored against ground truth in the one unit that matters: dollars of premium error. The vague question "which severity estimator?" becomes a measurable one.

The method under the microscope is Robust Quantile Least Squares (QLS) — percentile-matching grown up — measured against the estimator every actuary actually reaches for (the naive maximum-likelihood fit), its proper truncated/censored cousin, and the method of trimmed moments. Four falsifiable research questions, each with an honest prior written down before any code ran, keyed to the two ways real loss data is broken — contamination and incompleteness:

  • RQ1 — the price of robustness. On perfectly clean data, what does QLS cost versus the MLE? Prior: QLS is ~85–95% as efficient — premium error ~1.05–1.18× the MLE. A small premium for nothing gained yet.
  • RQ2 — breakdown. As a fraction ε of records become gross errors, does the naive MLE's premium diverge while QLS/MTM stay bounded? Prior: yes — MLE blows up, trimmed estimators cap the damage.
  • RQ3 — incompleteness. Under a $5k deductible (left truncation) and a $2M limit (right censoring), does observable-window QLS recover the truth where the naive full-sample MLE is biased? Prior: yes — naive MLE badly biased (>20% premium error); window fits recover.
  • RQ4 — the tail. If a heavy GPD fire tail is present and ignored, does the premium underprice materially? Prior: yes — an ignored tail underprices TVaR99 and the premium by >10%.

Every experiment is scored by one number: premium_error_pct = |fitted premium − truth premium| / truth premium × 100, lower is better. And there is a yardstick that governs the whole study: the Monte-Carlo floor. At the sample size used here (n = 2,000 claims), even a perfect, unbiased estimator posts about 4.28% mean premium error — pure finite-sample noise propagating through the nonlinear premium map. "0% error" is impossible; 5% is excellent. Every result below is read against that floor, never against zero.

Seven experiments later, all four questions have verdicts — two priors confirmed, one split down the middle, and one falsified twice. And the most valuable lesson is one nobody pre-registered: on this book the dominant pricing error is neither contamination nor truncation but fitting one curve to a mixture, and the cells that look most "accurate" turn out to be two large errors quietly cancelling.

The Method

If you have ever eyeballed a loss table and said "the median claim is about $8k and the 95th percentile about $100k, so a lognormal with those two percentiles fits" — you have already done a special case of QLS. That is percentile matching: pick the parameters that make the model's quantiles line up with the data's. Quantile Least Squares is percentile matching grown up: instead of matching two hand-picked percentiles exactly, fit the parameters so the model's quantile curve passes as close as possible to a whole grid of empirical quantiles, in a least-squares sense. It is, almost literally, ordinary least squares run on the quantile scale instead of the mean.

Why an actuary should care — three ways real loss data breaks

  • One bad record moves the MLE's premium; it barely moves a quantile you can see. The maximum-likelihood fit is a weighted average over every point, so a single fat-fingered "$5,000,000" drags the fitted σ — and therefore the tail, the TVaR, the premium — with it. An empirical quantile in the middle of the data does not budge when the largest point gets larger. QLS fits the quantiles you can trust and ignores the extremes by simply not putting grid points there.
  • A deductible is a left-truncation; a limit is a right-censoring. Below-deductible losses are never filed (truncated, not merely missing); above-limit losses are recorded at the limit (censored). QLS handles both by fitting only on the observable window and using the fact that d and u are known numbers.
  • Data errors are contamination. Real severity data is a clean model plus a small fraction of gross errors. QLS is built to have a bounded response to that fraction; the MLE is not.

The closed form — the whole estimator in three lines

A lognormal is location–scale in log space: with Z = log X ~ Normal(μ, σ), the quantile is linear in the standard-normal quantile zp = Φ−1(p):

Q_Z(p) = μ + σ·z_p          (z_p = Φ⁻¹(p), the standard-normal quantile)

So QLS on the log-losses is just ordinary linear regression of the empirical log-quantiles on zp: regress q̂(pi) on zp_i, and the slope is σ̂, the intercept is μ̂. No likelihood, no iteration — that is the entire estimator on clean, complete data:

z    = np.log(obs)                      # log-losses inside the window
qhat = np.quantile(z, p_grid)          # empirical quantiles on the grid
slope, intercept = np.polyfit(norm.ppf(p_grid), qhat, 1)
mu, sigma = intercept, abs(slope)      # intercept = μ̂, slope = σ̂

The observable window — how the known d and u earn their keep

Under a deductible d and limit u you observe X | d < X < u. The empirical quantiles of that sample estimate the conditional (truncated) quantiles, not the unconditional ones — fit them naively and you fit the wrong curve. But d and u are known, so the conditional model quantile can be written down exactly (log space, a = log d, b = log u):

Q_cond(p; μ,σ) = μ + σ·Φ⁻¹( Φ((a−μ)/σ) + p·[Φ((b−μ)/σ) − Φ((a−μ)/σ)] )

Fitting q̂(pi) ≈ Q_cond(pi; μ,σ) over the window identifies (μ, σ) from the middle alone — the known truncation points do the rest. As d → 0 and u → ∞ it collapses back to the simple μ + σ·zp. This is exactly what lets window-QLS recover the truth where a naive full-sample MLE, which pretends the truncated data is complete, is biased (Section 4, experiment E4).

GLS weights — and why the plug-in version disappointed

The efficient weights come from the joint asymptotic covariance of the sample quantiles (Adjieteh & Brazauskas 2025): for pi ≤ pj, Σij = pi(1−pj) / [f(Qi) f(Qj) n]. The generalized-least-squares fit solves min (q̂ − Qθ)T Σ−1 (q̂ − Qθ). In principle GLS beats OLS. In practice, the study's implementation uses the stable diagonal inverse-variance form (wi = f(Qi)2 n / [pi(1−pi)] from a first-stage OLS fit) — and it disappointed: on clean data it was less efficient than plain OLS (1.123× the MLE error vs OLS's 1.083×, E2). The first-stage plug-in of f and Q injects noise the 19-point grid cannot absorb; the full off-diagonal Σ−1 is the efficiency ceiling but ill-conditions on fine grids. The lesson lands in Section 6: reach for OLS, not the diagonal plug-in.

Robustness — trim the grid (but carefully)

Because each grid point is a middle order statistic, dropping the extreme pi makes the estimator ignore the tails where gross errors live — robustness by construction, not by down-weighting. Keep p ∈ [0.15, 0.85] and you have a bounded-influence fit. The catch this study discovers (E5): blind trimming also deletes real signal when the upper tail carries genuine risk — a hail shoulder is not contamination. The principled robustifier turns out to be the observable window, not the trim.

From parameters to the decision — the premium

Everything is scored through the layer payout Y = (min(X, u) − d)+. Truth and every fitted model are pushed through the same functionals, computed by 1-D integration of the survival function S (a finite interval, so finite even under a heavy tail):

E[Y]      = ∫_d^u S(x) dx                              (the stop-loss / layer identity)
VaR_p(Y)  = min(Q(p), u) − d
TVaR_p(Y) = VaR_p + 1/(1−p) · ∫_{d+VaR_p}^u S(x) dx
premium   = E[Y] + 0.10 · (TVaR₉₉(Y) − E[Y])       ← a 10% load on the TVaR₉₉ margin

The MTM cousin (Brazauskas, Jones & Zitikis 2009) trims moments instead of a quantile grid: symmetric-trim a fraction from each tail, then match the trimmed sample moments to the family's theoretical trimmed moments (closed form in log space for lognormal). QLS and MTM are siblings — both throw away the tails to bound influence; QLS works on the quantile scale and, via the window trick, handles truncation/censoring cleanly.

The efficiency side of the trade, measured (E2): QLS-OLS keeps ~85% of the MLE's premium efficiency; the diagonal plug-in GLS adds none; MTM's double trim costs the most.

The Data

The "data" in this study is code: generator.py's PVLossGenerator, a known-truth photovoltaic loss-severity lab. There is no external dataset and no download — because the whole point is a lab where the exact answer is known, so the DATA gate's usual go/no-go question ("is the data trustworthy?") becomes a sharper one: is the generator validated against its own ground truth? One sample is one loss year of a PV portfolio: n = 2,000 observed claims per replication, 200–1,000 Monte-Carlo replications per experiment, fully deterministic per seed.

The truth oracle — why this is a lab and not a guess

The engine that makes scoring possible is truth_functionals(d, u): the exact mean layer payout, VaR95/99, TVaR99, and risk-loaded premium, computed by 1-D numeric integration of the mixture survival function — and verified against an independent 106-draw Monte-Carlo within 1% (pinned in tests/test_rqls.py). Truth and every fitted model are priced through the same layer_functionals routine, so "truth" and "estimate" are never computed two different ways. When we say an estimator's premium error is 4.83%, we mean 4.83% away from a number we know exactly.

The Monte-Carlo floor — the yardstick for the entire study

Here is the discipline that keeps every later result honest. At n = 2,000, even a perfect, unbiased estimator does not score 0% premium error — it scores about 4.28% (sd 3.03), because finite-sample noise propagates through the nonlinear premium map (a mean, a 99th-percentile tail average, a loading). This is not bias; it is irreducible sampling error at this sample size. So the rule, set in the data card and obeyed in every figure: report every premium error against the 4.28% floor, never against zero. A "5% error" is near-perfect play; only a wildly larger number signals a real estimator problem.

The war story for a synthetic lab — truth drift, and the E1 gate

The framework's usual data war story — pandas string-dtype breaking dtype == "object" checks — is trivially absent here: every column is a generated numpy array with a declared dtype, no string-encoded booleans possible. The real GIGO risk for a synthetic lab is different and more insidious: silent truth drift. If the generator's parameters ever diverge from the documented truth, every conclusion is quietly wrong and nothing crashes to warn you. Three guards hold the line: truth functionals are integration-computed and Monte-Carlo-verified within 1% in the tests; the parameters are frozen in the data card and study.yaml; and experiment E1 is a hard truth-recovery gate — the naive MLE must recover a known single family before anything else runs. It did: μ̂ bias +0.00037 (2·SE = 0.00353) and σ̂ bias −0.00043 (2·SE = 0.00260), both comfortably inside tolerance. The generator recovers its own truth; the lab is trustworthy. Verdict: GO.

E1 — the gate (4.279%, keep)

Before anything else, the truth-recovery gate: the naive MLE on a known single-family lognormal(9.0, 1.1), n=2,000 × 200 reps, must come back unbiased or the whole lab is suspect. It did — μ̂ bias +0.00037, σ̂ bias −0.00043, both inside 2·SE — and its 4.279% premium error is not an error to fix but the Monte-Carlo floor itself: the irreducible n=2,000 sampling noise that every later result is measured against.

E2 — the price of robustness (4.459%, keep)

A paired 1,000-rep race on clean data set the efficiency number: the MLE floor refined to 4.116%, and QLS-OLS landed 4.459% = 1.083× it — robustness costs 8.3% more premium error when there is nothing to be robust against, exactly the small insurance premium the method card promised (RQ1 confirmed, low edge of the band). Two things surprised: the diagonal plug-in GLS was worse than OLS (1.123×), and MTM's double trim was the priciest of all (1.219×). Compute was a non-issue — ~6 seconds per 1,000 fits.

E3 — the breakdown, the money picture (49.97% reported, keep)

Now turn on contamination. Run as the first sanctioned sweep (20 cells → a sidecar, one ledger row = the pre-registered trimmed-QLS-OLS at ε=10%), E3 is the classic robustness exhibit. The naive MLE's premium error goes 4.21 → 21.28 → 45.47 → 136.59 → 352.19% across ε = 0,1,2,5,10% — even ε=1%, one bad record in a hundred, already puts it at 5× the floor. Trimmed QLS-OLS stays bounded: 5.42 → 6.25 → 8.58 → 20.32 → 49.97%. Both pre-registered falsification points cleared at ε=5% (MLE 136.6% > 50%; QLS 20.3% / MTM 22.7% < 25%): RQ2 confirmed. Note the direction — all estimators biased up: this contamination mix overcharges policyholders.

E4 — the mid-arc twist: wrong parameters, nearly right layer (4.834%, keep)

Here the study turns. Under $5k truncation + $2M censoring, window-QLS recovered the truth (4.834% ≈ the incomplete-data floor; trunc-MLE 4.680%) — the recovery half of RQ3, confirmed. But the other half of the prior — "naive MLE badly biased, >20% premium error" — was falsified, and the way it failed is one of the study's crown jewels. The naive MLE's parameter bias is enormous and theory-exact (μ̂ +0.596, the conditional-mean lift σ·λ(α)=0.593 at α=−0.439; σ̂ −0.347, the truncated-normal variance shrink). Yet its premium error is only 5.85% (signed −5.2%). The upward μ bias and the downward σ bias cancel in the layer's premium map — the naive fit approximates the conditional law above d, and a layer with deductible d only pays above d. Wrong parameters, nearly right layer. The picture below shows it directly.

E5 — window ≫ trim (18.78%, keep)

The realistic cell (full mixture, ε=5%, truncation + censoring) came with a 2×2 ablation that overturned its own pre-registered hypothesis. "Trimming matters most" was wrong; the window is what matters. Premium errors: window-only 8.90% ≫ window+trim 18.78% ≫ naive-QLS 23.74% ≫ trim-only 35.90%. Under a misspecified single-lognormal fit of a heavy-tailed mixture, the top quantiles carry real hail risk (×8, 8% of claims) — blind trimming of p > 0.85 deletes that signal and underprices the tail. The known (d, u) window is the principled trimmer: typos land below d, gross errors above u, so the policy terms screen contamination without touching real mid-tail signal. The best estimators at this cell are the two window-corrected, untrimmed ones (trunc-MLE 7.85%, window-QLS-untrimmed 8.90%).

E6 — the tail prior, falsified twice (25.81%, keep)

Turn on the heavy GPD fire tail (ξ=0.4 above $250k). RQ4 predicted an ignored tail would underprice the premium by >10%. It falls on both sides. Truth-side: because the $2M limit caps the layer, the entire GPD tail is worth only +0.174% of premium — a policy limit is itself tail protection, and tail shape beyond the cap cannot move a capped TVaR. Estimation-side: fitting the tail made things worse — peaks-over-threshold estimated ξ̂ = 0.217 (vs true 0.4, 36% of fits clipped) because the exceedances above the threshold are mostly hail mass, not fire GPD. And an emergent finding that becomes the study's biggest practical lesson: at ε=0 every single-lognormal estimator underprices this mixture layer by 23–26% — pure misspecification bias from the un-modelled hail component. The dominant "tail risk" here is hail, not the GPD splice.

E7 — the decision table, and "stopped-clock pricing" (18.78%, keep)

The finale consolidated everything into a decision grid (4 estimators × 5 contamination levels, 500 reps each) on the realistic lab — and reproduced E5's primary exactly (18.783886) as a free consistency check. The grid revealed the mechanism that ties the whole study together: at ε=0 every estimator underprices by 23–42% (misspecification), and contamination pushes premiums up, so as ε rises the two errors cancel. The cells that look "accurate" are two large wrongs meeting in the middle — stopped-clock pricing. Section 5 lays the full table out, with the money slide.

Total ledger wall-clock for all seven experiments: about five minutes of scipy. The phase budgets allowed 2.3 hours.

Findings & Insights

All four research questions closed with verdicts, and all four pre-registered predictions were graded. The full synthesis, with every number traced to results.tsv / aux_metrics.tsv / the sweep sidecars, is in findings.md; here are the load-bearing conclusions.

The RQ scorecard

  • RQ1 (efficiency cost at ε=0): CONFIRMED. QLS-OLS 4.459% = 1.083× the MLE floor (rel. eff 0.846 premium / 0.805 σ̂) — the small, promised insurance premium, at the low edge of the 85–95% band (E2).

Contamination pushes premiums up, so as ε rises the two errors cancel: the naive MLE crosses zero near ε≈2% (−3.5%) then goes catastrophically wrong (+188.9% at ε=10%); trunc-MLE sweet-spots at ε=5% (+2.1%); trimmed window-QLS drifts to near-exact at ε=10% (−0.6%) by cancellation, not by fit quality. Surprise 2 — parameter bias is not pricing bias

E4's naive MLE has a textbook-large, theory-exact parameter bias (RMSE ≈ 0.60 in μ, 0.35 in σ) yet prices the same-layer premium to within −5.2%. The upward μ bias inflates the layer mean; the downward σ bias deflates the tail; in this layer's premium map they nearly annihilate. The doctrine this sharpens is a warning: parameter RMSE is not pricing risk — a fit can be badly wrong in parameter space and nearly right in the premium you actually sell, and (elsewhere in the grid) the reverse. Always score the decision functional, never stop at the parameters.

Surprise 3 — the un-modelled mixture, not the estimator, is the real error

The severity fan makes the study's deepest lesson visual: every single-lognormal fit — robust or not — misses the hail shoulder, and the trimmed fits miss it worst (the −19% to −36% underpricing of E5).

What it is worth, in actuarial terms

The bankable outputs are three. (1) The decision table is a filing-memo artifact — a ready exhibit of "how wrong is the premium, and in which direction," for every estimator and contamination level — provided its caveat travels with it (the low-error cells are luck, not skill). (2) Robustness priced in dollars: 1.083× the MLE's clean-data cost buys a 7× smaller error at ε=10% (50% vs 352%, single family) and the difference between a −0.6% and a +188.9% premium on the realistic lab — for a TVaR-loaded rate, the gap between fileable and blown. (3) The synthetic-lab pattern itself: with no PV loss data, the study still produced quantified, defensible verdicts against a known truth — in about five minutes of scipy.

Model Coding Advice

This is the section that makes the study useful on Monday morning. The centerpiece code is two library-stable files — generator.py (the known-truth lab) and estimators.py (the four estimators) — plus a deliberately thin train.py that swaps one cell per experiment. Here are the four blocks worth stealing, then the pitfalls that shaped every finding above.

Block 1 — the truncated + censored likelihood

The proper conditional likelihood is what a naive MLE throws away. Uncensored points are conditioned on being above the deductible, so each contributes f(x)/S(d); censored points (recorded at the limit u, flagged) contribute S(u)/S(d); anything recorded at or below d can only be contamination and is dropped as unobservable under the truncated model:

def nll(theta):
    dist   = _frozen(family, theta)
    log_sd = np.log(dist.sf(d)) if d > 0 else 0.0    # log S(d): the truncation normalizer
    ll     = np.sum(dist.logpdf(unc)) - len(unc) * log_sd     # uncensored: f(x)/S(d)
    if n_cens:
        ll += n_cens * (np.log(max(dist.sf(u), 1e-300)) - log_sd)   # censored: S(u)/S(d)
    return -ll

res = minimize(nll, theta0, method="Nelder-Mead",
               options={"xatol": 1e-6, "fatol": 1e-8, "maxiter": 4000})

This is the estimator that won the realistic cell (E5, 7.85%). Its cost is speed: Nelder-Mead on this likelihood took 13.6 s of E4's 20.7 s wall-clock — the one place compute mattered. Note the 1e-300 floor inside the log: without it, a parameter draw that pushes S(u) to underflow crashes the optimizer mid-search.

Block 2 — the observable-window quantile grid (the QLS core)

Two branches. When the data is complete, QLS is the three-line closed form (a polyfit whose slope is σ̂). When a deductible or limit is present, match the empirical quantiles to the conditional model quantiles over the known window — the same identifiability trick from Section 2, in code:

qhat = np.quantile(z, p)                       # empirical quantiles of log-loss
full_line = (not isfinite(a)) and (not isfinite(b))   # a = log d, b = log u

if full_line and weights == "ols":            # complete data: the closed form
    zp = norm.ppf(p)
    slope, intercept = np.polyfit(zp, qhat, 1)
    return Fit("lognormal", {"mu": intercept, "sigma": abs(slope)})

def cond_q(mu, sigma):                         # windowed: CONDITIONAL model quantiles
    lo = 0.0 if not isfinite(a) else norm.cdf((a - mu) / sigma)   # F(d)
    hi = 1.0 if not isfinite(b) else norm.cdf((b - mu) / sigma)   # F(u)
    return mu + sigma * norm.ppf(lo + p * (hi - lo))
# fit: least_squares(lambda th: sw * (qhat - cond_q(th[0], exp(th[1]))), x0)

The lo + p*(hi−lo) re-maps each grid point onto the observable slice [F(d), F(u)] — the whole reason window-QLS recovers the truth (E4, 4.83%) where a naive fit bends off the diagonal. The obs = x[(~cens) & (x > d) & (x < u)] filter is the robustness: unit typos (×0.01) fall below d and gross errors (×10–100) mostly above u, so the known policy terms screen the contamination for free.

Block 3 — the GLS weights (and why they disappointed)

The efficient inverse-variance weights, from the asymptotic covariance of sample quantiles, plugged in from a first-stage OLS fit:

if weights == "gls":
    mu0, s0 = z.mean(), z.std(ddof=0)                  # first-stage OLS-ish fit
    q0   = mu0 + s0 * norm.ppf(clip(p, 1e-4, 1 - 1e-4))
    dens = norm.pdf((q0 - mu0) / s0) / s0              # f(Q_i) at the grid
    w    = dens**2 * n / (p * (1.0 - p))               # Σ⁻¹ diagonal (inverse variance)

The theory says this should beat OLS. It did not (E2: 1.123× the MLE error vs OLS's 1.083×). The plug-in of f and Q from a noisy first-stage fit injects more variance than the weighting removes on a 19-point grid; the full off-diagonal Σ−1 is the true efficiency ceiling but ill-conditions on fine grids. Practical rule: default to OLS. Only reach for full-covariance GLS on a coarse grid where the covariance inverts stably.

Block 4 — the premium functional (truth and fit, scored identically)

The single most important design choice in the lab: truth and every estimate are priced through the same routine, so a difference is never an artifact of two code paths. It is the stop-loss integral of the survival function, finite because the limit u caps the interval:

def layer_functionals(sf, qf, d, u, loading=0.10):
    mean_payout = quad(sf, d, u, limit=200)[0]              # E[Y] = ∫_d^u S(x) dx
    var99  = min(qf(0.99), u) - d if 0.99 > F(d) else 0.0
    tail   = quad(sf, d + var99, u, limit=200)[0]
    tvar99 = var99 + tail / (1.0 - 0.99)
    premium = mean_payout + loading * (tvar99 - mean_payout)
    return {"mean_payout": ..., "var99": var99, "tvar99": tvar99, "premium": premium}

Because the layer is capped at u, even a ξ=0.4 GPD tail integrates to a finite TVaR — which is precisely why the whole fire tail was worth only +0.174% of premium (E6). A policy limit is a tail-risk control written into the integral bounds.

The pitfalls list

  • Do not use the diagonal plug-in GLS. It added noise, not efficiency (E2: 1.123× > OLS 1.083×). Default to OLS; reserve full-Σ GLS for coarse grids.
  • Do not trim blind. On a mixture, the upper tail can be real risk, not contamination — trimming it deletes signal (E5: trim-only 35.90% vs window-only 8.90%). Let the observable (d, u) window do the screening; trim only what the window cannot reach.
  • Never evaluate on parameter metrics alone. E4's naive MLE had huge μ̂/σ̂ RMSE (0.60 / 0.35) yet a −5.2% premium error — the biases cancel in the layer map. Always push the fit through to the premium/VaR/TVaR you actually sell.
  • Model the mixture before blaming the estimator. Every single-family fit underprices this book by 23–42% at ε=0 (E6, E7) — misspecification, not estimation. A robust estimator cannot rescue the wrong family.
  • Guard the synthetic truth, and report against the MC floor. Gate the generator against its own truth before any experiment (E1: bias < 2·SE), and score every result against the 4.28% floor, never against 0 — or you will chase sampling noise as if it were bias.
  • Watch the contamination direction. This mix biases premiums up (overcharge); truncation-ignorance biases them down. Knowing the sign is half of reading the decision table.

Next Steps & References

This study's job — quantify, against a known truth, what robust quantile least squares costs and buys for a PV loss-severity premium under contamination and incomplete data — is done. The open questions for a follow-up, in priority order (from findings.md §7):

  1. Model the mixture (per-peril fits) — the structural fix. Every finding points here: the 23–42% underpricing at ε=0 (E6, E7) is misspecification, not estimation. Fit each peril (or a lognormal mixture with a heavy hail component) separately, price the layer on the mixture, and re-run the E7 grid. Prediction to falsify: the ε=0 column collapses toward the 4.28% floor and the "stopped-clock" cancellation disappears.
  2. An MLE-tc + window-QLS ensemble. Trunc-MLE won the realistic cell (7.85%, E5) but is slow and less contamination-immune at high ε; window-QLS is fast and bounded. A blend may dominate both across the ε grid — target the best per-cell error in the decision table.
  3. An ε-contamination stress-test template for filings. Package the E3/E7 sweep harness as a reusable exhibit: given a fitted severity model and a layer, report the signed premium / VaR / TVaR error across ε ∈ {0,1,2,5,10}% and flag any cancellation cell. This turns the study's method into a standard robustness appendix for a rate filing.
  4. Acquire real PV loss-severity data. The lab is calibrated to illustrative PV shape, not a real book. Even a modest real dataset (deductible/limit-layered claims) would let the estimators be compared on genuine severity — with the synthetic lab as the known-truth control for what "recovered the truth" should look like.
  5. GLS with the full covariance on coarser grids. The diagonal plug-in GLS added noise at 19 points (E2). The off-diagonal Σ−1 is the efficiency ceiling but ill-conditions on fine grids; a coarser 7–9 point grid with the full covariance may finally beat OLS.
