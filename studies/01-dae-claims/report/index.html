The Question

Picture the moment this study exists for. You are an actuary or a pricing data scientist, and someone — a conference talk, a Kaggle write-up, an eager vendor — tells you that self-supervised representation learning is what your claims models are missing. The star exhibit is always the same: Porto Seguro's Safe Driver Prediction, an auto-insurance claims competition won with a denoising autoencoder. Your data is auto-insurance claims. Should you invest?

Finding out alone is expensive. The method is genuinely unfamiliar territory for most actuarial teams: a neural net trained with no labels, a corruption scheme invented on a Kaggle forum, a representation you hand to a second model. Doing it credibly means a literature review, a correct implementation, honest baselines, and the discipline not to fool yourself — realistically weeks of a skilled person's time, with a real risk of an un-publishable "it kind of helped?" at the end. This study is that investigation run end-to-end under a research contract: 8 pre-registered experiments, every prediction written down before the run, about a minute of total model wall-clock — and a verdict an actuary can act on.

The decision under study: does a swap-noise denoising autoencoder (DAE) pay on 58,592 rows of weak-signal insurance claims — and if not for the headline metric, does it pay anywhere else? Four falsifiable research questions, each with an honest prior stated before any model ran:

  • RQ1 — the headline. Do frozen DAE representations, fed to LightGBM, beat the ancestor campaign's tuned raw-feature GBDT (val_auc 0.6701)? Prior: no — SSL pays at scale, and 58k single-modal rows is not scale.
  • RQ2 — additive value. Does concatenating the DAE representations with the raw features lift over 0.6701? Prior: ≤ +0.001 — trees already extract the axis-aligned signal the DAE re-expresses.
  • RQ3 — did it learn anything? Does a plain logistic regression on the frozen DAE representations beat the raw-feature LR floor (0.6255)? Prior: yes, modestly — the most likely "the DAE did something" signal.
  • RQ4 — the second acts. The same trained DAE can also impute missing cells (reconstruct them) and flag anomalies (reconstruction error). Do those carry independent value even if RQ1 says no?

The metric is validation AUC on a fixed, stratified 80/20 split (seed 42) — the identical split as the 215-experiment ancestor campaign, verified by a bit-exact anchor gate before anything else was allowed to run. Three campaign numbers serve as the reference lines throughout: the raw-LR floor 0.6255, the tuned single-GBDT 0.6701, and the cross-family soft-vote 0.6715.

Eight experiments later, all four questions have verdicts — and the most valuable result is one nobody pre-registered: the humble baseline that was supposed to be a floor turned out to be the ceiling.

The Method

If you know principal component analysis, you are two ideas away from a denoising autoencoder. This section teaches both ideas, then the two tabular-specific tricks (swap noise, RankGauss) that make the method work on policy data, then the honest story of where it came from — including the part of that story that predicts it may not work here.

Idea 1 — an autoencoder is nonlinear PCA

PCA finds a low-dimensional linear subspace that reconstructs your columns with least squared error. An autoencoder does the same job — compress each row, then reconstruct it — but the compressor (encoder) and reconstructor (decoder) are neural networks, so the "subspace" can bend. It can capture the curved, interacting structure of a policy record: that max_power, displacement, and segment move together; that certain region and vehicle combinations co-occur. Crucially it is trained unsupervised — no claim_status label anywhere. The hope is that the encoder's internal activations become a feature representation with the joint structure of the columns already baked in, which a downstream model can then exploit.

Idea 2 — denoising is what forces it to learn

There is a cheat available. Our network's hidden layers (256 wide) are wider than its input (94 dims) — it is overcomplete — so an ordinary autoencoder could simply learn the identity map: copy input to output, perfect reconstruction, nothing learned. The fix is the D in DAE: corrupt each input row, and demand the network reconstruct the clean row from the corrupted one. Now copying is useless — on the corrupted cells, the input is wrong. The only way to drive the loss down is to infer each corrupted cell from the other columns: if max_power is corrupted, recover it from displacement, segment, and model. Undoing corruption is learning the joint distribution of the columns. Formally, with corruption x̃ ~ q(·|x), the objective is:

minimize   E[ || g(f(x̃)) − x ||² ]        f = encoder, g = decoder
           reconstruct the CLEAN row x from the CORRUPTED row x̃

Swap noise — the tabular-native corruption

In images you corrupt by adding Gaussian noise or masking pixels, which is meaningful because neighboring pixels are correlated. A table has no such geometry — adding Gaussian noise to a one-hot fuel_type is nonsense. Michael Jahrer's answer (Porto Seguro, 2017) is swap noise: with probability p per cell, replace the value with the same column's value from a random other row:

x̃[i][j] = x[r][j]   with probability p   (r = a random donor row)
x̃[i][j] = x[i][j]   otherwise

The corrupted value is always a plausible value for that column — it came from a real policy — just wrong for this policy. That is exactly the corruption whose undoing teaches inter-column structure. Two study-specific routing decisions (Section 3 has the full table): the 17 is_* accessory flags are excluded from corruption (swapping a 0/1 accessory flag teaches nothing) and passed through untouched; and corruption is applied at the original-column level, then re-encoded — so a swapped categorical stays one valid one-hot, never a half-corrupted encoding (Section 6 shows the code). The swap rate p is a real hyperparameter: Jahrer used 0.15, and experiment E5 sweeps it rather than trusting him.

RankGauss — making MSE fair across columns

The reconstruction loss is a plain MSE over all encoded dimensions, so column scales matter: a raw region_density in the tens of thousands would dominate the loss while binary flags vanish. RankGauss fixes this by mapping each numeric column to a standard Gaussian by rank (sklearn's QuantileTransformer(output_distribution="normal")): the smallest value lands in the left tail, the median at 0, the largest in the right tail. Every numeric column then contributes to the MSE on equal terms. This detail is load-bearing — it is also, notably, most of what the supervised MLP in E2 needed to shine.

The deep-stack representation — what we actually hand downstream

After training, the decoder is thrown away. The feature handed to the downstream model is the deep stack: the concatenation of all three hidden-layer activations (3 × 256 = 768 dims), not just the last layer. Jahrer's observation was that earlier layers keep information the deepest layer has already abstracted away; stacking them beats any single layer. So the full pipeline is:

94-dim encoding:  21 RankGauss numerics  ⊕  17 is_* passthrough  ⊕  56 one-hot cat dims
encoder:          94 → 256 → 256 → 256   (ReLU), linear decoder head 256 → 94
training:         fresh swap noise each epoch (p = 0.15), MSE vs the clean row,
                  AdamW, early stop on a held-out reconstruction loss
feature out:      concat(h1, h2, h3) = 768 dims  →  LightGBM / logistic probe

The Porto Seguro story — told honestly

The precedent that makes this method worth testing is uncomfortably close to our problem: Jahrer's 1st-place solution to Porto Seguro's Safe Driver Prediction is also auto-insurance claim prediction. He won with a blend of six models — one LightGBM and five neural nets — and every neural net was trained on DAE hidden activations, not raw features. Swap noise at 0.15, RankGauss inputs, deep-stack features: the exact recipe above.

But three regime differences separate his setup from ours, and all three push against the DAE here:

The transductive point deserves a beat, because it is an integrity issue as much as a technical one: fitting the DAE on train+test features quietly uses the test distribution. Fine for a Kaggle leaderboard; not fine as a headline comparison against inductive baselines. This study's DAE is fit on the train fold only, with the fitted row count logged as a canary (n_fit_rows=46873), and the Jahrer-style transductive variant exists in the code only as a clearly-labeled aside. Representation learning's edge grows with unlabeled scale; at ~25× fewer rows and an honest inductive constraint, the method card's written prediction was that the DAE would not clear the tuned GBDT — and that a measured "no" would itself be the valuable answer. Section 4 shows what actually happened, including the result that made the question sharper than anyone planned.

The Data

Same book as study 00: the Kaggle litvinenko630/insurance-claims dataset — 58,592 policies, 45 prepared columns, binary target claim_status at 6.40% positive (3,748 claims), zero missing values, prepared by a prepare.py byte-identical to study 00's. The data gate's verdict was GO with no blockers; what is new in this study's data card is the DAE-specific column routing — because for an autoencoder, how each column enters the network is a modeling decision, not plumbing.

The 94-dimension routing table

dae.py routes every prepared feature column into exactly one of three encoders, for a measured input dimension of d = 94:

RouteColumnsEncoderDimsSwap-noise eligible?
RankGauss numerics 21 — all numerics except is_*: continuous (subscription_length, customer_age, region_density, dimensions, power/torque…), low-cardinality ordinals (airbags, ncap_rating…), and the 0/1 binary maps median-impute → QuantileTransformer(output_distribution="normal") 21 Yes
is_* passthrough 17 accessory/safety flags (is_esc, is_parking_sensors, is_brake_assist…) passed through as 0/1 17 No — deliberately excluded
One-hot categoricals 6 — region_code (22), model (11), engine_type (11), segment (6), fuel_type (3), steering_type (3) mode-impute → OneHotEncoder(min_frequency=20), dense 56 Yes (whole column swapped, then re-encoded)

Why the is_* flags are excluded from noise

Swap noise teaches by forcing the network to infer a corrupted cell from its neighbors. That only produces useful gradients when the cell has structure worth inferring. Swapping a 0/1 accessory flag mostly toggles a bit whose recovery signal is thin — a coin-flip-shaped pretext that adds loss noise without teaching joint structure. So the 17 flags ride along as clean context (the network may use them to reconstruct other columns) but are never themselves corrupted. The same 27 eligible columns (21 numerics + 6 categoricals) later define where E7 injects MCAR missingness — the imputation experiment deliberately mirrors the denoising doctrine.

The redundancy that becomes an asset

The data card's most interesting note: seven columns are near-deterministic functions of model (engine_type, displacement, cylinder, and the four torque/power columns) — 11 vehicle models, at most 11 distinct values each. For a GLM that is collinearity to manage; study 00 dropped them for its tree recipe. For a DAE this redundancy is exactly the inter-column structure the denoiser feeds on — a corrupted max_power is recoverable from model almost by lookup. The card kept all seven, and two experiments later confirm both faces of the coin: the redundancy is why the DAE imputes categoricals at 90% accuracy (E7) — and why handing trees the same information twice, raw plus re-encoded, actively hurts (E6).

Inherited guardrails, verified live

Two disciplines carried from study 00, both confirmed against this environment rather than assumed. First, the value-pattern check: under this machine's pandas 3.0.3, the 19 boolean-ish string columns report dtype str, not object, so a naive dtype == "object" gate would silently skip all 19 — prepare.py detects them by their actual value sets ({"Yes","No"}) and maps them deterministically. Second, the 6.4% imbalance is handled by the calibration doctrine, not re-weighting: every downstream classifier here trains with class_weight=None (E1's balanced setting exists only to reproduce the campaign anchor). The DAE itself is unsupervised — imbalance cannot bite it; it bites only the classifiers on top.

Row, column, and target counts match the ancestor campaign exactly — and the E1 anchor then proved it bit-exactly: val_auc 0.625462 vs the campaign's 0.6255, a gap of 0.000038. The split is the same split; every comparison downstream is legitimate. Full audit: data_card.md.

E1 — the gate (0.625462, keep)

Before any DAE work, the split-identity anchor: the campaign's LR+OHE baseline on the fixed split, required to land within ±0.001 of 0.6255 or the whole study stops. It landed 0.000038 off — and exactly matched study 00's own E1 to the sixth decimal, proving both studies share prepare.py and the split byte-for-byte. Every cross-study comparison in this report is legitimate because of this row.

E2 — the "floor" that became the ceiling (0.670616, keep — THE SURPRISE)

E2 was designed as a humble control: a plain supervised MLP (3×256 ReLU) on the DAE's exact 94-dim input encoding, so that E3-vs-E2 would isolate what self-supervision adds with the representation held fixed. The prediction: 0.63–0.65 — above raw LR, comfortably below the GBDT. The observation: 0.670616, +0.045 over the LR floor and half a point of the fourth decimal above the tuned GBDT's 0.6701. The control tied the champion. Twelve seconds of training on a laptop, early-stopped at epoch 15, with the study's best Brier (0.0587) and best decile lift (1.88) for good measure. Two consequences: the pre-registered keep bar for the DAE instantly hardened from "reach 0.6701" to "beat 0.670616" — and a meta-finding was born that outranks the study's official question (Section 5).

The infrastructure dragon — real research has one

E3's first run did not produce a number. It produced SIGSEGV, exit 139, no Python traceback, and an empty log file — the process died below Python at LGBMClassifier.fit, after the torch DAE stage had already succeeded. The armed prediction-collapse guard never fired; nothing Python-level ever ran wrong. Three isolation probes pinned the cause: (A) a torch-free process running LGBM on cached representations — fine; (B) one tiny torch-MPS op, then LGBM, same process — dead; (C) importing lightgbm first — survives toy loads, then dies inside the full-scale torch stage instead. Conclusion: torch and LightGBM each bundle their own OpenMP runtime (libomp) on macOS arm64, and whichever engages OpenMP heavily second segfaults the process; import order only picks the victim. The fix that carried the rest of the study is two-stage process isolation inside one train.py — a torch-only child fits the DAE and dumps caches, the lightgbm-first parent trains the head (Section 6 shows the code). Two ops corollaries came free: set -o pipefail (tee had masked the exit code) and PYTHONUNBUFFERED=1 (block-buffered stdout died with the process, which is why the log was empty).

E3 — the headline verdict (0.668271, discard)

With isolation in place, the RQ1 experiment ran clean: inductive DAE (swap 0.15, fit on 46,873 train-fold rows only), 768-d deep-stack, the campaign's LGBM recipe. Result: 0.668271 — dead-center in the predicted 0.66–0.67 band, 0.0018 below the tuned GBDT, and 0.0023 below E2's plain MLP on the identical encoding. That last comparison is the clean scientific one, and it is the study's climax: with the representation held fixed, self-supervision subtracted value. The unsupervised detour also cost 3× the wall-clock (37s vs 12s). RQ1: NO, as predicted — but against a harder bar than predicted.

E4 — but the DAE did learn something (0.658019, keep)

A plain logistic regression on the frozen 768-d representations scores 0.658019 — +0.0326 over the raw-feature LR floor, closing ~72% of the raw-LR→tuned-GBDT gap with a linear model. RQ3: YES. The representations demonstrably encode nonlinear joint structure a linear model can use. Both things are true at once, and this is the study's most instructive tension: the DAE learned real structure (E4), and that structure is worth nothing on top of plain supervised learning (E3 vs E2).

E5 — the sweep that falsified its own prediction (0.668271, discard)

The one sanctioned sweep: swap rate ∈ {0.10, 0.15, 0.25}, every trial logged to a sidecar, one ledger row. The pre-registered prediction — |Δ| ≤ 0.002 across rates, "swap rate is a minor lever at this scale" — was falsified: 0.10 costs −0.0063 and 0.25 costs −0.0041. Jahrer's 0.15 is a genuine local optimum on data 25× smaller than his — the one piece of the Porto Seguro recipe that replicated cleanly. The mechanism is visible in the DAE's own held-out reconstruction loss (rising 0.0154 → 0.0230 → 0.0358 with rate): at 0.10 the pretext task is too easy and applies too little denoising pressure; at 0.25 the input is over-corrupted. The winner (0.15) reproduced E3 bit-exactly — 0.668271 — which doubles as the determinism proof for the two-stage isolation. No improvement over E3, so by sweep rule 7: discard, honestly logged.

E6, E7, E8 — the second acts (Phase 2)

E6 (0.660653, discard): concatenating the raw 94-dim encoding with the 768-d representations — "give the tree everything" — did not merely fail to clear 0.6701; it landed 0.0076 below representations alone. The aux ledger shows why: LightGBM's best iteration fell from 107 to 55. With column subsampling at 0.7 over 862 largely redundant dimensions, the duplicated information dilutes every split's candidate pool and the model overfits earlier. A sharper null than predicted: trees do not want your re-expression of information they already have.

E7 (keep): the same frozen DAE, re-purposed as a missing-value imputer under injected MCAR missingness at 10% and 30%, against a train-fold median/mode baseline. Downstream AUC through a deterministically-refit E3 head: DAE wins at both rates, but barely (+0.0015 / +0.0013). At the cell level it is not close — numeric RMSE 1.02 vs 3.42 (3.4× better), categorical accuracy 90.1% vs 33.4% — the DAE is exploiting exactly the model-derivative redundancy the data card flagged. The predicted "gain grows with missing rate" clause was falsified (flat), because information loss itself (clean 0.6683 → 0.6322 at 30% MCAR) dwarfs the choice of imputer. Verdict: reach for the DAE-imputer when you need the values; the classifier's rank barely cares. (Figures in Section 6, where the write-back code lives.)

E8 (0.480548, discard): the last prior — reconstruction error as a calibration-free claim ranker, predicted lift@10% > 1.0 — was falsified with a twist: lift@10 = 0.934, and the ranker's AUC of 0.4805 means the signal is mildly inverted. The policies the DAE finds hardest to reconstruct — unusual vehicle-spec combinations — are marginally less claim-prone, because claims on this book ride weak exposure/usage signal (subscription length, age, density), not configuration weirdness. The same DAE that imputes 3.4× better than median (E7) is useless as an unsupervised claim flagger (E8): "unusual" and "risky" are different directions on personal-lines policy data.

Total ledger wall-clock for all eight experiments: about a minute. The phase budgets allowed three hours.

Findings & Insights

All four research questions closed with verdicts, and all eight pre-registered predictions got graded. The full synthesis, with every number traced to results.tsv / aux_metrics.tsv, is in findings.md; here are the load-bearing conclusions.

The RQ scorecard

  • RQ1 (DAE reps → LGBM vs tuned GBDT 0.6701): NO. E3 scored 0.668271 — −0.0018 vs the GBDT and −0.0023 vs E2's plain MLP on the identical encoding. The honest inductive DAE does not pay for the headline at 58k rows, exactly as the method card predicted.
  • RQ2 (DAE + raw concat): NO — and it regresses. E6 landed 0.660653, 0.0076 below reps-only. Redundant dimensions dilute the split budget (best iteration 107 → 55) and trigger earlier overfit. Sharper than the predicted "no lift."
  • RQ3 (linear probe vs raw-LR floor): YES. E4's logistic regression on frozen reps scored 0.658019 — +0.0326 over 0.6255, closing ~72% of the LR→GBDT gap linearly. The DAE genuinely linearized nonlinear structure.
  • RQ4a (imputer vs median under MCAR): YES — at the cell level. E7: numeric RMSE 1.02 vs 3.42 (3.4×), categorical accuracy 90.1% vs 33.4%; downstream rank gain only +0.0013–0.0015.
  • RQ4b (recon-error anomaly ranker): NO. E8: lift@10 = 0.934 < 1.0, ranker AUC 0.4805 — mildly inverted. Unusual ≠ risky on this book.

Surprise 1 — the meta-finding that outranks the official question

A plain 3×256 MLP — RankGauss + one-hot inputs, no class weighting, early stopping on validation AUC, 12.5 seconds on a laptop — ties the tuned GBDT (0.670616 vs 0.6701) and lands within 0.0009 of the campaign's cross-family soft-vote global best. Put that against the ancestor campaign's Phase 4, which spent 25 TabNet configs (best 0.6633) and 15 FT-Transformer configs (best 0.6695) to conclude "deep learning sits below the GBDT trio": the cheapest architecture beats both fancy ones — and the campaign never tried it. The nuance matters for anyone budgeting "deep learning for tabular": the value was never in the architecture; it is in the encoding (RankGauss), the capacity control (task-metric early stopping at epoch 15), and the calibration discipline (E2 also posts the study's best Brier, 0.0587). E2 does not overturn "trees win on medium tabular" — it ties, not beats — but it relocates where the DL money should go.

Surprise 2 — self-supervision subtracted value on this data

The clean comparison the study was engineered for: same 94-dim encoding, supervised MLP 0.6706 (E2) vs DAE→LGBM 0.6683 (E3) vs DAE→linear-probe 0.6580 (E4). The representations are real (E4 proves structure was learned) — but every pipeline that routed learning through the unsupervised pretext lost to direct supervised training, at 3× the compute. With all 46,873 training rows labeled and nothing else to learn from, the pretext task can only approximate what the label already teaches directly. SSL's regime — label scarcity, big unlabeled pools, multi-modality — is precisely what this dataset lacks, and the experiment quantifies the cost of ignoring that: −0.0023 AUC and 25 wasted seconds per run.

Surprise 3 — the dragon below Python

The study's operational lesson: torch and LightGBM cannot share a process on macOS arm64 (dual bundled libomp → SIGSEGV with no traceback; import order only picks the victim). Every guard the framework had — prediction-collapse checks, budget timeouts, status honesty — watches Python; this failure was below it. The durable fixes: two-stage process isolation (Section 6), set -o pipefail, PYTHONUNBUFFERED=1, and a bit-exact rerun as the determinism proof. Promoted to the framework's war-story canon as story #5.

What it is worth, in actuarial terms

Honestly: no pricing-metric lift. Nothing DAE-based improved rank, lift, or calibration over supervised baselines, so no rate filing or triage queue moves on these results. Model Coding Advice

This is the section that makes the study useful on Monday morning. The study's centerpiece code is dae.py — a ~380-line, sklearn-style SwapNoiseDAE — plus the two-stage launcher pattern in train.py that every torch+LightGBM experiment had to adopt. This walkthrough annotates the four blocks worth stealing, then the pitfalls that bit (or tried to bite) this study.

Block 1 — swap noise at the original-column level

The subtle design decision in the whole file. Corruption happens on the dataframe, per original column, and the corrupted frame is then re-encoded — never on the encoded matrix:

def apply_swap_noise(X, eligible_cols, *, rate, rng):
    """Swap-noise corruption at the ORIGINAL-column level."""
    Xc = X.copy()
    n = len(X)
    mask = np.zeros((n, len(eligible_cols)), dtype=bool)
    for j, col in enumerate(eligible_cols):       # is_* flags are NOT in eligible_cols
        flagged = rng.random(n) < rate            # each row corrupted w.p. rate
        mask[:, j] = flagged
        idx = np.flatnonzero(flagged)
        if idx.size:
            donors = rng.integers(0, n, size=idx.size)   # random donor rows
            col_vals = X[col].to_numpy()
            Xc.iloc[idx, Xc.columns.get_loc(col)] = col_vals[donors]
    return Xc, mask

Why it matters: corrupt the encoded matrix instead and a swapped categorical becomes a physically impossible half-corrupted one-hot (two hot bits, or none) — the network learns to denoise encoding artifacts, not policy structure. Original-column corruption guarantees every corrupted row is a valid row: a swapped fuel_type is still exactly one fuel type, a swapped numeric passes through the same RankGauss map as everything else. (Because both encoders are per-column deterministic maps, this is provably identical to copying the donor's encoded slice — the dataframe version is simply the honest, testable statement of the method.)

Block 2 — the training loop: fresh noise per epoch, index-shuffle batching

for epoch in range(self.max_epochs):
    # fresh swap noise EACH epoch (proper denoising training), then re-encode
    X_corrupt, _ = apply_swap_noise(X_tr, self.eligible_cols_,
                                    rate=self.swap_rate, rng=noise_rng)
    Z_corrupt = torch.as_tensor(self._encode(X_corrupt), device=self.device_)

    self.net_.train()
    for b in kleinlib.torch_loop.iterate_minibatches(   # MPS-safe: NEVER a DataLoader
        n_tr, self.batch_size, shuffle=True, generator=noise_rng
    ):
        bt = torch.as_tensor(b, device=self.device_)
        optim.zero_grad()
        recon = self.net_(Z_corrupt[bt])
        loss = loss_fn(recon, Z_tr_clean[bt])           # reconstruct the CLEAN row
        loss.backward()
        optim.step()

    # early stop on a FIXED held-out corrupted set — a stable signal across epochs
    with torch.no_grad():
        es_loss = float(loss_fn(self.net_(Z_es_corrupt), Z_es_clean).cpu())

Three deliberate choices: (1) fresh noise every epoch — the network must never memorize one corruption pattern; (2) index-shuffle batching via kleinlib.torch_loop, never a DataLoader/TensorDataset — on Apple MPS the obvious DataLoader route silently collapses predictions to a near-constant (the campaign's war story #2, and the reason min_proba_std is a hard guard); (3) the early-stop set is corrupted once, with a fixed seed — re-noising it each epoch would make the stopping signal jitter with the noise draw instead of tracking the model.

        raise RuntimeError(f"impute stage subprocess failed with rc={r.returncode}")

Rules of the pattern: the child never imports lightgbm; the parent imports lightgbm before anything torch-adjacent and never executes a torch op; everything crossing the boundary is CPU numpy in .pkl caches; and the child's return code is checked loudly. Run it under set -o pipefail with PYTHONUNBUFFERED=1, or a segfault will eat both the exit code (tee masks it) and the log (block-buffered stdout dies with the process). Cost of the pattern: one subprocess call. Proof it preserves determinism: E5's winner trial reproduced E3 bit-exactly (0.668271).

The pitfalls list

  • Never mix torch and LightGBM in one process on macOS arm64. Dual bundled libomp → SIGSEGV below Python; guards cannot catch it; import order only picks the victim. Two-stage isolate (Block 4). Detection recipe if you suspect it: (A) run the GBDT alone on cached inputs, (B) add one tiny torch op first, (C) flip import order — if A passes and B dies, you have it.
  • Never a DataLoader/TensorDataset on MPS. Index-shuffle batching (Block 2) plus a min_proba_std hard guard that raises on collapsed predictions. A collapsed model must crash loudly, not report a plausible-mediocre AUC quietly.
  • Corrupt original columns, not encoded ones (Block 1) — else you train the net to fix impossible encodings.
  • Sweep the swap rate. The "it's flat" assumption cost up to −0.0063 in this study's sweep (E5). Three points and a sidecar TSV is ~100 seconds of compute.
  • Log the inductive canary. Record n_fit_rows_ with every DAE result; it is the difference between a defensible number and a quiet transductive leak.
  • Run the supervised floor first. E2's 12-second MLP re-set this study's entire bar before the DAE ran. An SSL pipeline evaluated only against a linear baseline will look better than it is.
  • cw=None, calibrate after — for NNs too. Every class_weight=None arm here posted Brier ≈ 0.059; the lone balanced run (E1, anchor duty) posted 0.240. The doctrine transfers from GLMs to deep nets unchanged.
  • Pipe hygiene on every tee'd run: set -o pipefail and PYTHONUNBUFFERED=1. Without both, a hard crash leaves you a zero exit code and an empty log — the most expensive kind of nothing.

Next Steps & References

This study's job — quantify whether tabular self-supervision pays on this data, and where the same trained artifact pays anyway — is done. The open questions for a follow-up, in priority order (from findings.md §7):

  1. TabPFN v2, zero-shot. The knowledge base's default first move under ~50k rows, and at 58,592 rows this dataset sits at the edge of TabPFN v2.5's envelope. One inference pass answers whether a pre-trained tabular foundation model beats the E2 MLP / tuned GBDT pair (0.6706 / 0.6701) with zero tuning — the highest information-per-second experiment available.
  2. MLP + GBDT cross-family soft vote. E2's MLP is a genuinely different inductive bias at GBDT strength — exactly the diversity the campaign's soft-vote doctrine wants, where the FT-Transformer failed its correlation gate. Check the out-of-fold prediction correlation first (add the learner only if < 0.95), then target the campaign's 0.6715 global best.
  3. Complete the transductive aside. dae.py ships fit_mode="transductive" (the Jahrer-style train+val-features fit) but the ladder never ran it — the fairness rule kept it out of the headline and the study closed at 8/8. One clearly-labeled run would price exactly what the "Kaggle-style" peek is worth at 58k rows. Prediction to falsify: +0.001–0.003 over E3's 0.668271, still below E2.
  4. Entity embeddings for region_code. The one higher-cardinality column (22 levels) was one-hot everywhere here. A small nn.Embedding inside the E2 MLP tests the embeddings-beat-encoding claim far below its usual >1000-level rule — cheap, and it upgrades the architecture that actually won.
  5. Productionize the imputer under MAR, not just MCAR. E7's 3.4× win was measured under uniformly-random masking. Real missingness is MAR-at-best (spec fields absent conditional on model/segment). Re-run the E7 harness with MAR injection keyed on model before recommending the DAE-imputer for a production data-quality workflow.
