The Question

Before you trust any research framework to price real risk, it has to earn that trust on a problem whose answer you already know. That is what this study is: a proving-ground reproduction. We take a mature 215-experiment campaign on the Kaggle auto-insurance-claims dataset, and we ask whether this framework — a fresh port called kleinlib plus the disciplined edit-run-log loop — can hit the campaign's known-good anchor numbers on the nose.

Framed as a decision an actuary actually faces: can I rely on this pipeline's numbers, and does its headline modeling doctrine hold on my data? The study breaks that into two falsifiable research questions.

  • RQ1 — Split identity. Does prepare.py plus the fixed train/validation split reproduce the campaign's anchor scores exactly? If the preprocessing or the split has drifted even slightly, every later comparison is poisoned — so this is a hard gate, not a nicety. The prediction: the logistic-regression baseline lands at val_auc = 0.6255 ± 0.001, and if it misses, we stop everything.
  • RQ2 — The calibration-first doctrine. On a target that is only 6.4% positive, does training on the natural class balance and calibrating the probabilities afterward (class_weight=None + isotonic regression) beat the reflexive fix of re-weighting the loss (class_weight="balanced")? The campaign says re-weighting wins nothing on ranking and wrecks the probabilities. We test that claim head-on, on this study's own anchor configuration.

The metric under study is validation AUC (higher is better) — the one number every experiment optimizes. Everything else (Brier score, log-loss, decile lift, wall-clock) is recorded on the side and used to tell the fuller story. Six experiments later, both questions have a verdict — and the most instructive result is the one anchor that refused to reproduce on the first try.

The Method

The workhorse here is logistic regression — and if you price with GLMs, you already know it. It is the same machinery as your Poisson-frequency or Gamma-severity models, just with a Bernoulli response and a logit link, aimed at P(claim) instead of a count or an amount. No new mathematics; the skill is in how you feed it.

The linear predictor, and why it is both blessing and curse

The model scores each policy on the log-odds scale with a plain weighted sum:

η = β₀ + β₁·x₁ + β₂·x₂ + … + βₖ·xₖ      (the linear predictor)
P(claim) = 1 / (1 + e^(−η))                (the logit link squashes it into [0,1])

Each coefficient β₃ is a log odds-ratio: holding everything else fixed, a one-unit move in x₃ multiplies the odds of a claim by e^β₃. An underwriter — or a regulator — can read the coefficient table straight off. That transparency is the GLM's blessing.

The curse is the flip side of the same coin. Because the score is a straight sum, the model is monotone and additive by construction: it literally cannot see a U-shape, a threshold, or an interaction between two features unless you build that shape into the design matrix yourself. A boosted tree discovers curvature and interactions on its own; a GLM never will.

Why splines — the model's only path to a curve

So we hand the model its non-linearity. The standard tool is a spline basis: instead of feeding customer_age as one column, you expand it into a handful of smooth basis functions, and the GLM fits a separate weight to each. The sum of those weighted pieces is a smooth curve in age — risk that rises, plateaus, and falls — which a single linear term could never express. Splines on the two or three most important continuous features are mandatory feature engineering for a linear model on this data (worth about +0.026 AUC in this study; see the journey). This is the exact opposite of the rule for trees, where hand-built features mostly just add noise.

Two details decide whether splines actually pay off, and neither is captured by the phrase “5 knots” — they are the traps this study fell into and climbed out of (Section 6):

  • Where you place the knots. knots="quantile" puts them where the data actually lives; the sklearn default "uniform" spaces them evenly and wastes resolution on empty ranges.
  • Whether you include a bias column. include_bias=False, because the regression already has its own intercept — the default duplicates it and quietly degrades the fit.

Why calibration comes first on a 6.4%-positive target

Here is the idea that trips up most newcomers: ranking and calibration are not the same thing. AUC only asks “did the model put riskier policies above safer ones?” A model can rank acceptably and still be badly biased in the actual probability it reports — and for pricing, the probability is the product.

The instinct on an imbalanced target is to re-weight the loss so the rare positives “count more” (class_weight="balanced"). Do not. Re-weighting barely moves AUC and it systematically inflates the predicted probabilities — the model now thinks claims are far more common than 6.4%. The campaign found this across every re-weighting trick it tried, and this study reproduces it live (Section 5). The right recipe:

  1. Train on the natural distribution: class_weight=None. Don't lie to the model about the base rate.
  2. Fix the probabilities afterward with isotonic regression (CalibratedClassifierCV(method="isotonic", cv=5)) — a monotone step function that re-maps raw scores onto the observed claim frequencies. It preserves the ranking and repairs the bias. This is the actuarially load-bearing step.
  3. Tune the decision threshold last, for whatever operating point the business needs.

Put together, the whole model is a short pipeline — the encoder, spline, and calibration choices are the experiment; everything else is library:

from sklearn.preprocessing import OneHotEncoder, SplineTransformer
from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression
from sklearn.calibration import CalibratedClassifierCV

# OHE is non-negotiable for nominal categories in a linear model (never ordinal):
#   OHE 0.6255  vs  ordinal 0.4039  -- a wrong encoder makes LR unusable.
pre = ColumnTransformer([
    ("cat", OneHotEncoder(min_frequency=20, handle_unknown="infrequent_if_exist"), cat_cols),
    ("num", SplineTransformer(n_knots=5, degree=3, knots="quantile", include_bias=False), spline_cols),
])
glm = Pipeline([("pre", pre), ("lr", LogisticRegression(C=1.0, class_weight=None, max_iter=2000))])
model = CalibratedClassifierCV(glm, method="isotonic", cv=5).fit(X_tr, y_tr)

One honest caveat, so you set expectations correctly: the signal in this dataset is weak. The score histograms for the two classes overlap almost completely — the best any linear model reaches is val_auc ≈ 0.65, and a tuned boosted tree only gets to ~0.66. There is no configuration that makes this an easy separation problem; the GLM's job is a transparent, well-calibrated anchor, not a leaderboard win.

Full pedagogy for the method lives in the study's method_card.md and the knowledge base's method_cards/glm-pricing.md; this section is the working actuary's version.

The Data

The dataset is the Kaggle litvinenko630/insurance-claims file: 58,592 policies, 41 raw columns (45 after prepare.py), with a binary target claim_status that is 6.40% positive (3,748 claims). Those three numbers — row count, prepared column count, and positive rate — match the campaign's own documented figures exactly, which is the first data-integrity signal, before the modeling even starts.

The war story that gate 1 exists to prevent

Here is the trap, and it is not a museum piece — it reproduced live in this environment. Nineteen columns in this data are really booleans wearing string clothes: the seventeen is_* feature flags hold exactly "Yes" / "No", and two more (rear_brakes_type, transmission_type) hold "Disc"/"Drum" and "Automatic"/"Manual". The obvious way to find categorical columns is to check the dtype:

# The bug. Looks reasonable. Silently wrong.
categorical = [c for c in df.columns if df[c].dtype == "object"]

Under this machine's pandas 3.0.3, every one of those nineteen columns reports its dtype as str — not "object". Pandas 3.0 made a dedicated string type the default storage, so the label changed. The check above matches none of them, silently skips all nineteen boolean features, and hands the model a pile of raw "Yes"/"No" text. In the ancestor campaign this cost about two hours of confusion before anyone noticed the features had gone missing.

The fix is a discipline, not a patch: never key on the dtype label; inspect the actual values.

# The fix. Detect by VALUE PATTERN, not by dtype.
def detect_yes_no_columns(df):
    out = []
    for col in df.columns:
        vals = set(df[col].dropna().unique())
        if vals <= {"Yes", "No"}:          # membership on the real values
            out.append(col)
    return out

yes_no_cols = detect_yes_no_columns(df)     # finds all 17, dtype label be damned
for col in yes_no_cols:
    df[col] = df[col].map({"No": 0, "Yes": 1}).astype("int64")

After conversion, prepare.py verifies all nineteen are int64 with zero nulls across the 58,592 rows. The same principle handles the numbers-in-strings columns (max_torque = "113Nm@4400rpm"), which are regex-parsed into clean numeric columns rather than treated as free text. This value-pattern discipline is exactly why the E1 anchor could reproduce the campaign's prepared frame bit-for-bit.

The go / no-go call

Gate 1 ranks every issue and blocks modeling until the verdict is GO. Here there were three WARN-severity issues and no blockers:

  • WARN — the 19 string-boolean columns above. Mitigated by the value-pattern conversion; verified int64 post-fix.
  • WARN — seven vehicle-spec columns (engine_type, displacement, cylinder, and four parsed max_torque_*/max_power_* columns) are near-deterministic functions of model — redundant, and a source of collinearity for a GLM. Kept for the LR anchors (to reproduce the campaign exactly), dropped for the HGBT recipe.
  • WARN — the 6.4% class imbalance. Handled not by re-weighting but by the calibration doctrine (Section 2), which RQ2 puts directly to the test.

No leakage column, no broken split, no unusable encoding once the value-pattern conversions are applied. Verdict: GO. The full audit is in data_card.md.

Read left to right, the trajectory tells the story: two flat LR anchors, a jump to the HGBT anchor, a calibration test that trades a hair of AUC for a huge probability improvement, the LR recovery that finally hits its target, and a small sweep nudge to the study best.

E1 & E3 — the anchors that landed bit-exact

E1 (LR + one-hot + class_weight=balanced) came in at 0.625462 against a target of 0.6255 — off by 0.000038, inside a tolerance ten times looser. The split-identity GATE passed almost to the digit, which means kleinlib and prepare.py are a faithful port. E3, the tuned HGBT anchor, was even tighter: 0.662897 vs 0.6629, off by 0.000003. E3's recipe was recovered verbatim by git show-ing two committed campaign experiments — when the exact code survives in git, reproduction is trivial and perfect.

E2 — the anchor that would not reproduce (the star of the show)

Then E2 broke the streak. The target was the campaign's winning Phase-1 LR at 0.6528. E2 assembled what the campaign's ledger described — splines on three columns, a log-transform, two interactions, isotonic calibration — and landed at 0.625533. That is −0.0273 off target, and worse, it was below its own splines-only ablation (0.627959). Stacking on more “correct” features made the model worse, not better.

Why couldn't we just copy the recipe? Because the campaign's entire Phase-1 spline block (its experiments 40–48) is discard-status with no surviving commit. Under the very same commit-or-revert discipline this framework enforces, a discarded experiment's code is reverted and never committed — so all that remains is the one-line prose description in the ledger. E2 was reconstructed from prose alone, and prose, it turns out, is lossy.

Seven reconstruction variants were tried; none closed the gap. Sweeping the knot count all the way to 50 hit a ceiling of ~0.641, still 0.012 short. The honest conclusion at the time: the historical “+0.025 HUGE” jump could not be reproduced from the documented recipe, and E2 was logged as a keep with the miss recorded plainly, not smoothed over.

E4 — the calibration doctrine, proven in a single A/B

E4 paused the reconstruction to settle RQ2. It is E1's exact pipeline with one lever flipped: class_weight="balanced" becomes class_weight=None plus isotonic calibration. Validation AUC barely moved (0.622859, down just 0.0026) while the Brier score improved ~4× (0.240153 → 0.059279) and log-loss ~2.9× (0.672617 → 0.232385). Textbook doctrine confirmation: re-weighting bought nothing on rank and had been quietly wrecking the probabilities.

E5 — the redux that cracked E2 open

E5 went back to E2 with a better method: instead of reconstructing from prose, it git-recovered the campaign's last committed LR base and re-applied the delta chain on top. It landed at 0.651707 — −0.0011 from the 0.6528 target, essentially a hit, and +0.0262 over E2. What closed the gap was three implementation details that no prose description ever mentioned: quantile-placed knots, no spline bias column, and isotonic cv=5 rather than cv=3. That E5's Brier (0.058960) and log-loss (0.229111) match the campaign's own reported 0.059 / 0.229 almost to the digit is the proof it was a genuine recovery, not a lucky number. The full lesson is in Section 5.

E6 — the sweep demo, and the study best

E6 exercised the framework's one sanctioned escape-hatch, the sweep protocol, tuning the HGBT learning_rate over five values. The winner (0.06) reached 0.664322, +0.001425 over E3 and the study best. Every trial was logged to a sidecar; only the winner earned a ledger row and a pickled model. A small, honest gain — and Section 5 is candid about how little that +0.0014 is worth for actual pricing.

Findings & Insights

Both research questions closed with a verdict. The full synthesis, with every number traced to results.tsv and aux_metrics.tsv, is in findings.md; here are the load-bearing conclusions.

The verdicts

  • RQ1 (split identity): CONFIRMED — wherever the exact recipe is recoverable. Two anchors landed bit-exact (E1 off by 0.000038, E3 off by 0.000003), and the third (E2) reproduced to within 0.0011 once E5 recovered it from git rather than prose. The framework port is faithful; the lone miss was about recoverability, not correctness.
  • RQ2 (calibration-first doctrine): CONFIRMED. E4's single-lever A/B bought a ~4× Brier improvement (0.240153 → 0.059279) for a −0.0026 AUC cost, and E5 independently produced the study's best Brier of all six runs (0.058960) on a calibrated LR. Re-weighting the loss is calibration poison; train on the natural rate and calibrate afterward.

The reliability diagram is the doctrine made visible. A well-calibrated model tracks the diagonal — when it says “8% risk,” about 8% of those policies actually claim.

Surprise 1 — the compressed-description trap (a framework lesson)

The headline finding is not about insurance at all; it is about how research records itself. E2 reproduced the campaign recipe perfectly at the lever level and still missed by 0.027, because a one-line ledger entry — “splines(5 knots, deg 3) + log1p + interactions + isotonic” — names the levers but not the dozen constructor kwargs that decide whether a lever delivers +0.025 or +0.0025. Three of them were the whole story:

  1. knots="quantile" — not sklearn's default "uniform";
  2. include_bias=False — the default duplicates the model's intercept;
  3. CalibratedClassifierCV(cv=5) — not cv=3.

The mechanism, and the rule that falls out of it: a git show of a keep commit recovers every choice exactly and for free (that is why E1 and E3 were trivial); prose cannot. Keep-chain commits are the real configuration record — descriptions compress, and the compression is lossy precisely on the non-default kwargs that move the number. The cheapest fix in the whole framework is to spend two extra words in a ledger description on the non-default kwargs (splines(quantile knots, no bias)); those two words would have made E2 exact on the first try.

Surprise 2 — the best-AUC model is not the best-calibrated model

E6 has the study's top AUC (0.664322) but a Brier of 0.222681 — 3.8× worse than E5's calibrated 0.058960 — because every HGBT run here is uncalibrated. Ranking and calibration trade off, so “best model” is use-dependent: E6 for pure ranking, E5 for a number you can put in a rate. (No run tripped the prediction-collapse guard; the calibrated LRs' low score-spread is isotonic compressing toward the 6.4% base rate, which is correct, not a failure.)

Model Coding Advice

This is the section that makes the study useful on Monday morning. It walks the actual winning train.py (the E6 state, committed at 3f3822f), then names the three details that bit the LR reconstruction, then the workflow discipline that kept the whole thing honest.

The winning train.py, annotated

The first thing to notice is how little code an experiment is. Everything stable — data loading, the fixed split, metrics, model saving — lives in kleinlib, so train.py is a thin, readable surface. The knobs sit right at the top:

RANDOM_SEED = 42
EXPERIMENT_ID = 6          # bump per experiment; matches the results.tsv row
MIN_FREQUENCY = 20         # campaign OneHotEncoder rarity threshold

# 7 columns that are near-deterministic functions of `model` (see data_card issue #2) --
# dropped for the HGBT recipe as a documented ablation.
DROP_REDUNDANT = [
    "engine_type", "displacement", "cylinder",
    "max_torque_nm", "max_torque_rpm", "max_power_bhp", "max_power_rpm",
]

The data path drops those redundant columns and then hands off to the fixed split — the same seed, ratio, and stratification every experiment, because comparability depends on never re-shuffling:

def load_split():
    X, y = kleinlib.data.load_xy(PREPARED_PATH, TARGET_COLUMN)
    X = X.drop(columns=DROP_REDUNDANT, errors="ignore")
    return kleinlib.data.fixed_split(X, y)   # seed=42, test_size=0.2, stratify=True

The model itself is exp 3's HGBT recipe with exactly one number changed — the sweep winner's learning_rate=0.06 (was 0.05). That one-line delta is the experiment:

def build_model(numeric_cols, categorical_cols):
    preprocessor = kleinlib.encoders.build_preprocessor(
        numeric_cols, categorical_cols, kind="ohe", min_frequency=MIN_FREQUENCY
    )
    classifier = HistGradientBoostingClassifier(
        learning_rate=0.06,        # <-- the only change from exp 3 (was 0.05)
        max_iter=500, max_leaf_nodes=31, l2_regularization=0.0,
        random_state=RANDOM_SEED, class_weight="balanced",
        early_stopping=True, validation_fraction=0.1, n_iter_no_change=20,
    )
    return Pipeline([("preprocess", preprocessor), ("model", classifier)])

And main() never touches results.tsv or the metrics itself — it fits, then hands the fitted model to kleinlib.eval.evaluate, which computes every metric, writes the aux sidecar, and (if it is a new best) pickles the model. The experiment author writes none of that plumbing:

def main():
    X_tr, X_va, y_tr, y_va = load_split()
    numeric_cols, categorical_cols = kleinlib.data.feature_column_groups(X_tr)
    model = build_model(numeric_cols, categorical_cols)
    model.fit(X_tr, y_tr)
    kleinlib.eval.evaluate(model, X_va, y_va, exp_id=EXPERIMENT_ID,
                           metric_name="val_auc", metric_goal="higher", study_dir=".")

The three details that bit the LR reconstruction — copy these exactly

The E6 file above is a tree, so it does not show them, but the LR recovery (E5) turned on three constructor kwargs that look optional and are not. If you build splines for a linear model, set all three deliberately — leaving them at sklearn's defaults cost 0.026 AUC (the entire difference between the failed E2 and the recovered E5):

SplineTransformer(
    n_knots=5, degree=3,
    knots="quantile",       # place knots where the data lives (default "uniform" wastes them)
    include_bias=False,     # the regression already has an intercept; the default duplicates it
)
CalibratedClassifierCV(glm, method="isotonic", cv=5)   # cv=5 per the campaign's best-practices doc, NOT cv=3

The lesson generalizes past these three: when a result depends on a non-default kwarg, that kwarg is load-bearing and belongs in the record. Which brings us to the workflow.

The workflow discipline that kept it honest

  • Commit-or-revert first, then exactly one results.tsv row. Never the other way around, and never a batch of rows. The row points at the commit that produced it, so the ledger is always an accurate index into git. This is also what let E1 and E3 reproduce trivially — the code was there, in a keep commit.
  • A discard is a discard. Reverted, uncommitted. That honesty is correct — but it is also exactly why the campaign's Phase-1 recipe (all discards) could only be reconstructed from prose. If a discarded recipe will matter later, write its two or three non-default kwargs into the description text before you revert.
  • Don't re-run for cosmetics. The aux sidecar is append-only with no dedup; re-running train.py just to refresh a log file double-writes its metrics block (this study hit exactly that and had to hand-clean it). Once a result is recorded, leave it.
  • Sweeps go through the one escape-hatch. Every trial to a sidecar TSV, the winner snapshotted back into train.py as a plain diff, re-run once to confirm, then committed — one ledger row for the winner (E6). No unattended meta-runners.

Next Steps & References

This quickstart's job — onboard the framework, prove split identity, confirm the calibration doctrine — is done. The open questions for a follow-up study, in priority order:

  1. Elastic-net on the spline basis. E5's design matrix (quantile-knot splines plus interactions) is wide. An L1+L2 penalty (saga solver) could prune the redundant basis columns and test whether the 0.651707 LR ceiling is real capacity or just overfit — the cheapest way to try to beat E5 without hand-selecting features.
  2. Target encoding (with smoothing) for LR. The knowledge base puts smoothed target encoding within ~0.002 of one-hot overall and ahead of it on the high-cardinality region_code (22 levels, only ~41 validation rows). A clean A/B against E1's OHE on the high-cardinality columns is worth a run.
  3. Monotone-constrained HGBT. The campaign's strongest single model used monotone constraints with the “advanced” method. Porting actuarially-signed monotonicity onto the E6 HGBT would test whether the +0.0014 sweep gain compounds with monotonicity — and, importantly, yields a filable tree.
  4. TabPFN v2, zero-shot. At 58,592 rows this dataset now sits inside TabPFN v2.5's supported range. A zero-shot pass is a cheap, high-information probe of whether a pre-trained tabular foundation model beats the tuned GBDT (E6) with no tuning at all — the regime shift the knowledge base flags as the big one since 2024.
  5. Graduate to studies 01 and 02. The anchors validated here become the baselines for studies/01-dae-claims (representation learning) and studies/02-rqls-pv-severity (severity / regression), where the genuinely open methodological questions live.
