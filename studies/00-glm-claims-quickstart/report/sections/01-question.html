The Question

Before you trust any research framework to price real risk, it has to earn that trust on a problem whose answer you already know. That is what this study is: a proving-ground reproduction. We take a mature 215-experiment campaign on the Kaggle auto-insurance-claims dataset, and we ask whether this framework — a fresh port called kleinlib plus the disciplined edit-run-log loop — can hit the campaign's known-good anchor numbers on the nose.

Framed as a decision an actuary actually faces: can I rely on this pipeline's numbers, and does its headline modeling doctrine hold on my data? The study breaks that into two falsifiable research questions.

  • RQ1 — Split identity. Does prepare.py plus the fixed train/validation split reproduce the campaign's anchor scores exactly? If the preprocessing or the split has drifted even slightly, every later comparison is poisoned — so this is a hard gate, not a nicety. The prediction: the logistic-regression baseline lands at val_auc = 0.6255 ± 0.001, and if it misses, we stop everything.
  • RQ2 — The calibration-first doctrine. On a target that is only 6.4% positive, does training on the natural class balance and calibrating the probabilities afterward (class_weight=None + isotonic regression) beat the reflexive fix of re-weighting the loss (class_weight="balanced")? The campaign says re-weighting wins nothing on ranking and wrecks the probabilities. We test that claim head-on, on this study's own anchor configuration.

The metric under study is validation AUC (higher is better) — the one number every experiment optimizes. Everything else (Brier score, log-loss, decile lift, wall-clock) is recorded on the side and used to tell the fuller story. Six experiments later, both questions have a verdict — and the most instructive result is the one anchor that refused to reproduce on the first try.
